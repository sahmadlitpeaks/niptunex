The science, step by step

How NIPTune X works

A single maternal blood sample contains tiny fragments of fetal and maternal DNA. We sequence them, analyse them, and translate the result into clinically meaningful insights.

The science

What's in a maternal blood sample

During pregnancy, fragments of DNA from the placenta circulate in the mother's bloodstream alongside her own DNA. Modern sequencing lets us read both — separating fetal signals from maternal ones with high precision.

  • Cell-free fetal DNA (cffDNA)Placental DNA fragments — typically 10–20% of total cell-free DNA — which we use to screen for chromosomal conditions.
  • Maternal genomic DNAExtracted from the buffy coat fraction, genotyped on a curated SNP panel for pregnancy-related risk variants.
  • Internal QC controlsFetal fraction estimation, sequencing depth, and assay-level controls — every report includes the underlying quality metrics.
In the laboratory

Our analytical pipeline

Each sample passes through a controlled, traceable workflow with documented QC at every stage.

Stage 1

Extraction

Plasma is separated and cell-free DNA is purified using validated automated chemistry.

Stage 2

Library prep

Indexed sequencing libraries are prepared for both the cfDNA and maternal genomic pools.

Stage 3

Sequencing

Paired-end whole-genome low-pass sequencing on Illumina NovaSeq 6000.

Stage 4

Analysis & report

Bioinformatic risk classification + clinician-reviewed report generation.

What to expect

Possible outcomes & next steps

Low risk

The vast majority of results indicate a low probability for the screened conditions. Routine antenatal care continues as planned.

High risk

A high-risk result is not a diagnosis. Your clinician will discuss confirmatory testing (e.g. amniocentesis or CVS) and counselling options.

Inconclusive

Occasionally the fetal fraction is too low for a definitive call. We offer a complimentary re-draw, typically two weeks later.

Important. NIPTune X is a screening test. It estimates risk and does not replace ultrasound, clinical evaluation, or, where indicated, confirmatory diagnostic testing. Always discuss results with a qualified healthcare professional.
